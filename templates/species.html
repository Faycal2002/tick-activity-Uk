Tick prevention & protection

Simple habits can greatly reduce the risk of tick bites and tick-borne diseases for both pets and people.

Before going outdoors
  • Use vet-recommended tick treatments on pets.
  • Wear long sleeves and trousers in tall grass.
  • Choose light-coloured clothing to spot ticks easily.
After walks & trips
  • Check your dog, cat, and family for ticks.
  • Pay attention to ears, neck, between toes.
  • Shower soon after outdoor activity if possible.
If you find a tick
  • Remove with a proper tick remover or fine tweezers.
  • Pull steadily, close to the skin—do not twist or burn.
  • Contact a vet or doctor if you notice illness or rash.

High

Currently recorded {{ counts.get("Dermacentor frontalis", 226) }} sightings. Often associated with birds and can occasionally bite pets or humans, especially in green urban areas.

  • Hosts: Passerine birds, small mammals, occasionally pets
  • Activity: Mainly spring to early summer
  • Regions: Widely distributed across UK cities
Fox/badger tick

Ixodes canisuga

High

Currently recorded {{ counts.get("Ixodes canisuga", 210) }} sightings. Closely linked to wild canids (foxes) and badgers, but may also be found on dogs that roam in woodland or semi-rural environments.

  • Hosts: Foxes, badgers, dogs
  • Activity: All year, with peaks in spring/autumn
  • Regions: Hotspots around suburban and rural edges
Southern rodent tick

Ixodes acuminatus

Medium

Currently recorded {{ counts.get("Ixodes acuminatus", 192) }} sightings. Primarily associated with small rodents, but can occasionally be found on pets that explore dense vegetation or overgrown gardens.

  • Hosts: Rodents, small mammals, sometimes cats and dogs
  • Activity: Warmer months, especially late spring
  • Regions: Scattered across multiple UK locations
Marsh tick

Ixodes apronophorus

Low

Currently recorded {{ counts.get("Ixodes apronophorus", 186) }} sightings. Favours damp, marshy or wetland habitats. Less frequently encountered in typical urban parks but still relevant near waterlogged areas.

  • Hosts: Small mammals, wildlife, occasionally dogs
  • Activity: Spring and early summer
  • Regions: Localised around wetter landscapes
Tree-hole tick

Ixodes arboricola

Low

Currently recorded {{ counts.get("Ixodes arboricola", 186) }} sightings. Mainly associated with birds nesting in tree cavities. Considered lower risk for routine pet exposure, but still part of the UK tick landscape.

  • Hosts: Birds, especially cavity nesters
  • Activity: Follows bird breeding seasons
  • Regions: Woodlands and mature tree areas
